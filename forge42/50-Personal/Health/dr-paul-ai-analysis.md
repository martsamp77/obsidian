## Optimization Opportunities

### 1. Glycine Dose Is Sub-Therapeutic

You're taking 500mg. Sleep studies showing efficacy use **1-3 grams**. At 500mg, you're likely getting minimal benefit. Glycine works via NMDA receptors and peripheral vasodilation to promote sleep onset. It also improves next-day cognitive performance when sleep is restricted.

**Fix:** Increase to 2-3g nightly. Glycine powder is cheap and dissolves easily — you could add it to your magnesium glycinate water.

### 2. Missing Vitamin K2 — Critical Gap

You're taking 10,000 IU Vitamin D3 + cod liver oil (additional vitamin D) + 1,000mg calcium. This combination without Vitamin K2 (specifically MK-7) is a known concern:
- Vitamin D increases calcium absorption from the gut
- Calcium supplements flood the blood with calcium
- Without K2, that calcium can deposit in arteries (calcification) rather than bones
- K2 activates osteocalcin (directs calcium to bones) and Matrix GLA protein (prevents arterial calcification)

Given your already-compromised cardiovascular lipid profile, **arterial calcification risk from unsupported calcium + D3 is a real concern.**

**Fix:** Add Vitamin K2 (MK-7) 100-200 mcg daily, taken with your Vitamin D3 (both fat-soluble, morning with food).

### 3. Get Your 25(OH)D Tested

You're taking 10,000 IU vitamin D3 (2.5x the tolerable upper intake level) plus additional vitamin D from cod liver oil, and you have **never tested your serum vitamin D level.** This is a blind spot. Studies show 10,000 IU daily can cause:
- Hypercalciuria in ~31% of users
- Mild transient hypercalcemia in ~9% of users
- Target serum 25(OH)D is 40-60 ng/mL for optimal health; levels above 100 ng/mL are potentially toxic

You may be perfectly fine — many people are deficient even at high doses — but you genuinely don't know. This is one of the easiest things to test and resolve.

### 4. Free T3 Is Low-Normal — Possible Energy Factor

Your Free T3 at 2.6 pg/mL sits in the lower quarter of the reference range (2.0-4.4). T3 is the active thyroid hormone that drives metabolic rate, energy production, and body temperature regulation.

**Carnivore diet connection:** Very low carbohydrate intake can impair T4-to-T3 conversion, since the liver's conversion process is partly insulin-dependent. This is a documented phenomenon on ketogenic and carnivore diets. Your TSH is normal (1.49) and Reverse T3 is normal (19.7), which suggests the pituitary isn't alarmed — but a low-normal Free T3 can still contribute to:
- Lower subjective energy
- Slightly lower metabolic rate (harder to lose weight)
- Cold sensitivity

**Recommended:** Discuss with Dr. Saya whether optimizing T3 is warranted. Some TRT clinics add low-dose T3 (liothyronine 5-12.5 mcg) for patients with low-normal Free T3. Alternatively, some carnivore practitioners find that adding small amounts of carbohydrate (fruit, honey, rice) around workouts improves thyroid conversion without leaving the diet framework.

However, a well-designed human study found no significant effects on gut microbiome or glucose tolerance at the maximum acceptable daily intake. At 3 packets/day, you're well below concerning levels. This is low priority, but if you're looking for marginal gains: stevia or monk fruit are better-studied alternatives with potential metabolic benefits.

## Kidney Health Note

Your eGFR has fluctuated (97 -> 82 -> 76 -> 86) and BUN is intermittently elevated. On a strict carnivore diet, this deserves ongoing attention:
- High animal protein intake increases intraglomerular pressure and renal blood flow by ~30%
- Chronic metabolic acidosis from sulfur-containing amino acids (abundant in red meat) can promote renal fibrosis over time
- The absence of fruits and vegetables removes alkaline-buffering salts

Your current eGFR of 86 is acceptable, and the BUN elevation is likely dietary. But the downward trend from 97 to 76 over two years (before rebounding) is worth monitoring. A cystatin C-based eGFR is a more accurate measure for high-protein dieters than creatinine-based eGFR, since creatinine is a byproduct of muscle metabolism and can be artificially elevated by meat consumption.
